04Reasoning

What should happen next

Interpretive Synthesis · Procedural Memory

Synthesizes conceptual meaning, analytical logic, and situational context into explanation, recommendation, prioritization, and action. The quality of reasoning is determined entirely by the richness of what it can reason over. The substrate shapes the intelligence.

Augmentation Required
  • Bind output to full stack state at moment of reasoning
  • Record uncertainty and contested inputs explicitly
  • Capture actors and decision authority
  • Enable backward traversal across market cycles

The reasoning layer is where large language models, agent frameworks, and orchestration engines operate. Without memory augmentation, every recommendation it produces is disconnected from the prior recommendations made on the same asset, the same portfolio, or the same market condition. A recommendation produced today has no durable connection to the one produced eighteen months ago. Those two moments are unrelated — until decision lineage binds them.

03Context Graph

What is happening around us

Situational Truth · Episodic Memory

Connects entities, events, actions, provenance, and outcomes into a living structure. The difference between knowing a metric changed and understanding the full chain of events that produced the change.

Augmentation Required
  • Explicit temporality — moment of decision, not just present
  • Decay states — Active, Archived, Deprecated lifecycle
  • Provenance tags at every third-party handoff
  • Event chains preserved across system and manager changes

The context graph as currently framed captures current situational truth. Episodic memory requires it to be explicitly temporal — not just what is happening, but what was happening at the moment a decision was made. The property management transition. The deferred maintenance call. The competitive incentive campaign. These facts shape decisions — and they require lifecycle governance so they transition from Active to Archived rather than remaining perpetually current or being deleted entirely.

02Semantic Layer

How things are measured

Analytical Truth · Semantic Memory (Quantified)

Governs metrics, KPIs, calculation logic, aggregations, and reusable business rules. Ensures analytical consistency — when two agents ask the same question, they get the same number, from the same logic.

Augmentation Required
  • Calculation lineage — formula version at time of computation
  • Metric tagging — which formula produced which number
  • Reconciliation audits to surface silent drift
  • Counterparty definition mapping with provenance

"Renewal rate" sounds unambiguous. In practice it requires governed answers to at least five sub-questions: Does it include option exercises? What is the eligible lease cohort? What time window applies? How are partial renewals counted? Are cause terminations excluded? Two analysts applying different defaults produce numbers that look comparable but aren't. The Drift Register catches this — but only if the semantic layer has been designed to surface formula versions alongside metric values.

01Ontology

What things are

Conceptual Truth · Semantic Memory

Defines canonical objects, types, hierarchies, attributes, and relationships. Makes the domain legible to both agentic systems and people. A property in a loan portfolio and one in asset management may share an address but represent structurally different entities.

Augmentation Required
  • Version history — prior definitions preserved, never overwritten
  • Definition provenance — who, when, under what authority
  • Plural ontologies — counterparty definitions with governed mappings
  • Drift detection registered across periods and partners

Plural ontologies replace the failed mandate of one canonical model. In a distributed operating model — with outsourced property managers, JV partners, and multiple systems of record — forcing a single definition is often neither practical nor correct. The ontology layer should instead allow different valid definitions to coexist with explicit scope tags, provenance, and governed mappings between them. Standardization becomes a service offered, not a mandate imposed.

Real Estate Application
RE Application

Each Layer in a Real Estate Intelligence Stack

Reasoning layer: An agent recommends holding a logistics asset through the current rate cycle. That recommendation is only trustworthy if it can be traced to the discount rate methodology in use, the occupancy definition applied, and the competitive context visible at the moment of synthesis — not the context that exists today.

Context graph: Tracks that a property management transition occurred at a specific asset in March 2024, that a deferred HVAC decision was logged in June 2024, and that a competitor ran a 3-month free rent campaign in the submarket from August through October 2024. The renewal rate decline in Q3 2024 is only explainable when these three facts are visible, connected, and preserved.

Semantic layer: "Tenant" in a CBRE-managed office portfolio means something structurally different from "tenant" in a Greystar-managed multifamily asset. "Property" in Yardi may not resolve to the same entity as "property" in Argus or VTS. The ontology makes these distinctions explicit — and plural ontologies let a JV partner's definitions coexist with yours without silent collision.

The Dependency Chain The reasoning layer's quality is entirely determined by what it can reason over. If the ontology has drifted, the semantic layer has inconsistent formulas, and the context graph has stale facts — the reasoning output is wrong at scale. Fast and authoritative, but wrong. This is the real risk of deploying AI without the augmented architecture.
