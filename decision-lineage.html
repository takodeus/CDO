iv · Decision Lineage

Not a log.
A reconstructable chain.

Logs record what happened. Decision lineage records why it made sense, and preserves the chain of reasoning, definition, measurement, and context that produced a judgment. It is not a fifth layer sitting above reasoning. It is a cross-cutting thread that runs through all four simultaneously.

Data lineage tells you where a number came from. Decision lineage tells you why it meant something at the moment it was used.

Rules vs. Decision Traces Rules tell a system what should happen in general. Decision traces capture what happened in this specific case — which definition was active, which policy version applied, which exception was granted, who held authority, and what precedent was cited. Agents don't just need rules. They need access to the record of how rules were applied in the past: where exceptions were granted, how conflicts were resolved, and which precedents actually govern reality rather than what the policy document says. That record is what decision lineage is. Without it, agents can follow rules consistently but cannot navigate the exceptions and precedents that make those rules operational in practice.

The IC Reconstruction Problem

A fund is reviewing a logistics acquisition approved in Q2 2023. The IC wants to understand whether the original underwriting held, whether the thesis was sound given what was known at the time, and whether similar assumptions should inform the follow-on vehicle.

With data lineage: they can find the approved model file and trace the numbers. They know where the exit cap rate came from in the spreadsheet.

With decision lineage: they can reconstruct the occupancy definition used, the cap rate methodology in force at that moment, the market comparable set that grounded the exit assumption, the macro thesis articulated by the AM team, the three specific risks the IC flagged as acceptable, and the two risks they considered but judged immaterial. The difference between a file retrieval and a genuine strategic review.

The Practical Question It Enables "In Q2 2023 when we approved this acquisition, we used a 7.5% exit cap rate. The context that justified that assumption included a specific set of tenant credit profiles, a specific macro rate outlook, and a specific view of submarket supply. How many of those conditions still hold? Where have they changed? And therefore. How much of the original thesis is still valid?" This question is unanswerable without decision lineage.

The Regulatory Reconstruction Problem

A consumer lender deploys an AI model to support credit underwriting decisions in Q1 2024. The model runs on a feature store built on Snowflake, with features derived from bureau data, internal transaction history, and behavioral signals. It is accurate, well-calibrated, and in production.

In Q3 2025, a regulatory examination begins. The examiner asks a question that should be straightforward: for the applications declined in Q2 2024, what features drove the model’s recommendation, which version of the model was active, what training data vintage underpinned the feature definitions, and how did the protected attribute exclusion logic function at that specific point in time?

The answer requires reconstructing the state of a system — not as it is today, but as it was at the moment of each decision. The model version is known. The feature store schema from Q2 2024 is partially recoverable. The training data vintage is in a Git tag. The exclusion logic, however, was updated in a hotfix deployed in late Q1 2024. Whether the hotfix was active for a given application depends on the deployment timestamp, which is not bound to individual inference records.

The data lineage is intact. The decision lineage is not.

The documented failure pattern In October 2025, Deloitte Australia submitted a government report riddled with AI-generated errors and agreed to refund part of the $290,000 contract. The 237-page report contained fabricated academic references, nonexistent research papers, and invented quotes from federal court judges. The failure is structurally identical: a fluent output, produced by a capable model, that cannot be traced to the premises that produced it. One case ends in a regulatory inquiry. The other in a news story. The architectural failure is the same.
