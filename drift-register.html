v · Drift Register

Semantic fidelity
across space

Drift is not a data quality problem. It is a meaning problem. The same term comes to mean different things in different places, without anyone deciding that it should. A drift register makes that divergence visible before it compounds into a misaligned IC presentation, an LP report discrepancy, or an AI recommendation built on incompatible inputs.

Why Drift is Nobody's Fault

Drift accumulates through legitimate local decisions that, in aggregate, erode shared meaning. A property manager onboards with different default reporting logic. A new analyst applies a slightly different cohort definition. A JV partner's system uses a different fiscal year convention. None of these are errors.

All of them are drift events. And without a register, they are invisible until they collide, in a board presentation, an LP report, or an AI agent recommendation that blends incompatible inputs from systems that were never reconciled.

The failure mode isn't that someone was wrong. It's that two teams were right about different things, and the system had no mechanism to surface the divergence.

Traceable Coexistence, Not Forced Convergence

Resolution does not always mean convergence on one canonical definition. In a distributed operating model with outsourced managers, JV partners, and multiple systems of record, forcing a single definition is often neither practical nor correct.

The alternative is traceable coexistence: plural definitions with explicit scope, provenance, and governed mappings between them. The drift register records which definition was active, where, and when, so that cross-portfolio comparisons can be made correctly even when the underlying definitions differ.

Standardization becomes a service offered, not a mandate imposed.

Real Estate Application
RE Application

Where Drift Enters a Real Estate Portfolio

Every handoff in the real estate operating model is a potential drift event. When property operations are outsourced to JLL, their definition of "occupancy" defaults to physical occupancy. The fund's reporting layer uses economic occupancy. Both are defensible. Neither is communicated. Over two reporting cycles, the gap accumulates.

When a JV partner onboards with their own Argus templates, their definition of stabilized NOI excludes management fees above-the-line. Fund accounting includes them. The blended portfolio NOI is systematically inconsistent, and the inconsistency is large enough to affect cap rate calculations, valuation conclusions, and carry waterfall projections.

The Visibility Problem These gaps are not detectable by data quality checks. Every row is present. Every field is populated. The pipeline is green. The problem is semantic, and it only surfaces when someone compares two numbers that should be comparable but aren't. By then, it has usually appeared in an LP report, an IC memo, or an AI recommendation.

Occupancy Rate Open · High Impact ▼
Economic occupancy: rent collected ÷ gross potential rent. Excludes concessions. Weighted by gross leasable area.
Physical occupancy: executed leases ÷ total units/SF. Includes leases within free rent period. Unweighted count.
Q3 2024, surfaced during LP reporting cycle review
3.8 percentage point gap across the Sun Belt multifamily portfolio (91.2% vs 87.4%)
Business Impact LP quarterly report showed 91.2% occupancy. Property manager dashboard showed 87.4% for the same portfolio. Two LPs submitted written inquiries. The IC presentation for the follow-on fund used the physical figure. The fund model used the economic figure. Waterfall projections were based on incompatible inputs, producing a carry calculation that was overstated by approximately $2.1M.
Resolution Path Semantic governance council convened. Formal version boundary established: all fund-level reporting uses economic occupancy from Q4 2024 forward. Property manager dashboards retain physical occupancy with explicit scope tag. Governed mapping created between the two definitions. Prior-period figures retroactively labeled with definition provenance tag.

NOI before management fee, excluding capex reserves. Treats tenant improvement allowances as below-the-line items.
NOI after management fee, including a 15¢/SF capex reserve. TI allowances amortized above-the-line over lease term.
Q1 2025, flagged during annual valuation review process
Varies by asset: 8–14% gap. Office-heavy assets show widest divergence due to TI treatment difference.
Business Impact Cap rate calculations in AM reports are based on a systematically higher NOI figure than fund accounting. Asset-level IRRs appear stronger than fund-level IRRs not due to performance differences but due to definition differences. The gap has been consistent enough that teams have learned to expect it without understanding why, which is exactly how invisible drift becomes structural.
Resolution Path (Active) Working group formed with AM and Fund Accounting. Interim measure: all cross-portfolio comparisons to include definition footnote specifying which NOI convention is in use. Target: reconciled definition or formal traceable coexistence protocol established by Q3 2025, with retroactive application to 2023 vintage fund modeling.

The other half of the problem The Drift Register governs semantic fidelity across space — the same term meaning different things in different places at the same time. Decay Governance governs semantic fidelity across time — context that was once correct becoming incorrect as the conditions it described change or resolve. They are two dimensions of the same failure. A complete memory architecture requires both. The next page addresses the temporal dimension.
